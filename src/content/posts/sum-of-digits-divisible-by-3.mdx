## Motivation

My friends invited me to lunch at the Google office today (very fortunate for a man who's fending on his own). While eating, we somehow ended up debating the supposed superiority of the dragon over the hare in the Chinese zodiac. As a loyal hare, I came to the defense of hares everywhere by claiming that 1999 is a far more interesting number than the boring 2000. For whatever reason, this led me to ask whether 1999 is divisible by 3. It *felt* like it should be. (Of course it isn’t—1999 is prime and the concatenation of three 9s, a number which is the square of another great prime. What a fantastic number!)

Two friends at the table immediately said no, and I was impressed by how quickly they answered. When I asked how they knew, they replied, “Didn’t you ever learn the trick where you sum the digits and check whether that sum is divisible by 3?” I hadn’t. To test it, I tried 201: $2+0+1=3$, and indeed it’s divisible by 3. Same with 2001. The rule seemed to work, but when I asked *why*—*warum muss das sein?*—no one at the table had an answer.

We must roll up our sleeves so as to determine **why is must be**.

## Proof

We will show that a number is divisible by 3 if and only if the sum of its digits is divisible by 3. We begin with the two-digit case and then generalize. (We ignore the one-digit case because it is trivial.)

### Two-Digit Number

Let a two-digit number be written as $d_1d_0$ in base-10, meaning

$$
10d_1 + d_0.
$$

Let

$$
x = d_1 + d_0,
\qquad
y = 10d_1 + d_0.
$$

Assume that $x$ is divisible by 3, so $x = 3k$ for some integer $k$. Then we may rewrite

$$
d_0 = x - d_1.
$$

Substituting into the expression for $y$, we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
y &= 10d_1 + (x - d_1) \\
  &= 9d_1 + x.
\end{aligned}
$$

Both terms on the right-hand side are divisible by 3: $x = 3k$ by assumption, and $9d_1 = 3(3d_1)$. Therefore,

$$
y = 3(k + 3d_1),
$$

which shows that $y$ is divisible by 3.

Thus, for a two-digit number, divisibility by 3 is equivalent to the divisibility by 3 of the sum of its digits.

### The General Case

Now consider an $n$-digit number written as

$$
d_{n-1}d_{n-2}\ldots d_0,
$$

which represents the integer

$$
y = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} 10^i d_i.
$$

Let the sum of its digits be

$$
x = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} d_i.
$$

Assume that $x$ is divisible by 3, so $x = 3k$ for some integer $k$.

We separate the $i=0$ term in the expression for $y$:

$$
y = d_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} 10^i d_i.
$$

From the definition of $x$, we have

$$
d_0 = x - \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} d_i.
$$

Substituting this into the expression for $y$ yields

$$
\begin{aligned}
y
&= x - \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} d_i + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} 10^i d_i \\
&= x + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} (10^i - 1)d_i.
\end{aligned}
$$

For each $i \ge 1$, we may write

$$
10^i - 1 = 9(10^{i-1} + 10^{i-2} + \cdots + 1),
$$

which is a multiple of 9 and therefore a multiple of 3. Hence, each term $(10^i - 1)d_i$ is divisible by 3, and so is their sum.

Since $x = 3k$ is also divisible by 3, it follows that $3 \mid y$, another notation to say "3 divides $y$". This completes the proof for the general case.

## Conclusion

I am now rather satisfied with this answer, and it will be a technique I use to quickly check divisibility by 3. Naturally, I am now curious what other similar shortcuts exist, besides the obvious divisibility by 2 or 5 where we need only check the last digit.
